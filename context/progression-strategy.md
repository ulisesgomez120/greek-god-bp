## Tips for Using These Tutorials

1. **Start Light**: Always begin with lighter weights to master the form before progressing
2. **Focus on Form**: Proper technique is more important than lifting heavy weights
3. **Watch Multiple Videos**: Different instructors may explain concepts in ways that click better for you
4. **Practice**: Watch the video, then practice the movement without weights first
5. **Progress Gradually**: Increase weight only when you can perform the exercise with perfect form
6. **Ask for Help**: Don't hesitate to ask a trainer at your gym to check your form

## Safety Reminders

- Always warm up before exercising
- Use proper safety equipment when available (safety bars, spotters, etc.)
- Listen to your body and stop if you feel pain
- Stay hydrated and rest between sets
- Consider working with a certified personal trainer when starting out

The Progression Strategy: How to Get Stronger and Bigger
The program's method for driving progress is progressive overload, which is the gradual increase of stress placed on the body. However, how you apply this principle changes based on your experience level.
The progression is broken down into three distinct stages:

1. Untrained Individuals (Less than 3 months of consistent training):

- The Goal is Technique, Not Weight. The absolute priority is to learn and perfect exercise form. You should use the same weight for at least 3 weeks.
- Progression is measured by your confidence and technical proficiency, not by the numbers on the bar. Adding weight too soon with poor form is the fastest way to get injured and halt progress.

2. Beginners (3-9 months of consistent training):

- The Goal is Linear Progression. Once your form is solid, you can start making consistent, small increases in weight.
- This means adding a small, fixed amount of weight (e.g., 5 lbs / 2.5 kg) to your main lifts each week. This simple, linear model is extremely effective for beginners whose bodies adapt quickly.

3. Early-Intermediates (9-18 months of consistent training):

- The Goal is RPE-Based Progression. At this stage, weekly strength gains will naturally slow down. Instead of forcing weight increases, you will use the Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE) scale to guide your progress.
- RPE is a 1-10 scale rating how difficult a set was (10 is absolute failure, 9 means you had one rep left, 8 means you had two reps left, etc.).
- You will aim for the RPE target listed for each exercise. You only increase the weight on an exercise when the RPE for your working sets drops by one point. For example, if you successfully complete 3 sets of 8 reps at 150 lbs with an RPE of 8, you will continue using 150 lbs until you can perform that same workout at an RPE of 7. At that point, you have earned the right to increase the weight for your next session. This method auto-regulates your training, ensuring you only add stress when your body is ready for it.
